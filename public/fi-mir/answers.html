02 Consider Pivoting

I am not pivoting the company. I am sharpening the wedge, and the sharpening is the important update. The starting frame was broad: Quartermint as treasury, cards, compliance, and filings across the seven political entity types, "Brex for Public Affairs." That is still the long-term shape. But "do everything for everyone" is not a wedge, and the feedback at hotseats and Office Hours kept landing on the same point: name the one customer and the one problem you win first. The sharpening, decided with my paying design partner after the Epic Sprint interviews: - One customer: the compliance operator who runs federal House and congressional committees, plus the candidate who acts as their own treasurer, which turned out to be a surprisingly large underserved segment (when Maryland overhauled its filing system, about half the treasurer-training attendees were candidates acting as their own treasurer). - One problem: producing a footed, line-level-accurate FEC filing from messy real inputs (ActBlue, the bank, scanned checks) without a human losing the weekend to it. - One deliberate exclusion list: not state filings, not Senate (the hard-money and soft-money reconciliation needs humans), not qualitative strategy work inside the compliance tool. Those are later, or separate products. Why this and not the broad platform: the filing is the recurring, deadline-driven, legally mandatory job an operator will pay for every month. It is automation, not AI: the inputs already exist, so the report falls out. Win the committees on the filing, and the filing history becomes the switching cost that lets the rest of the platform follow. Is the market big enough: a real operator told me a $1,000/month compliance product is something he could "sell to 20 races easily," and the incumbent (Capital Compliance) charges roughly $2,500/month for a full retainer. The room to undercut on price and win on product is real, confirmed by buyers, not assumed. So: same company, same long-term vision, much sharper first customer and first problem.

06 Challenge Your Revenue Model

My current primary model is a per-committee compliance subscription at $1,000/month. The assignment asks me to challenge it with two other models, so here are the two real alternatives, stress-tested. Alternative 1: Transaction / interchange revenue (basis points on money flowing through a deposit-bank card and payments partnership, e.g. Amalgamated). Positives: - Scales with customer success, not sales headcount: revenue grows as the customer's money moves, with no per-seat negotiation. - Aligns with the bank distribution thesis: the bank already earns on deposits and interchange, so a revenue share is a natural structure. - Low friction at point of use: it feels free to the customer, no monthly invoice to approve. Negatives: - Requires the bank partnership to be signed and live before a dollar is earned, a long pole that is partly outside my control. - Optics and compliance scrutiny: taking basis points on political money movement invites regulatory and journalist questions a flat fee does not. - Lumpy and seasonal: political money spikes before elections and goes near-zero after, so it is run-rate, not recurring. Top competitors using this model: ActBlue and Anedot (processing fees on contributions), Stripe-based political processors, and deposit banks like Amalgamated and Chain Bridge that monetize interchange directly. Realistic 18-month customers + strategy: 10 to 20 committees with banking inside the partner rails, because each requires the operator to move their banking. Strategy: land them on the SaaS filing product first, then migrate banking once trust exists. Two price points: (a) market parity: the standard ~2.2 to 2.9% card-processing rate, of which only a thin share would accrue to Quartermint; (b) price-to-$1M: a blended ~1.5% all-in fee where Quartermint keeps the full margin, which would require roughly $67M of routed spend in 18 months, far beyond reach at this customer count. Verdict: real long-term upside, wrong as the near-term primary model. Alternative 2: Done-for-you managed compliance retainer (priced like the incumbents, ~$2,000 to $2,500/month, higher touch). Positives: - Highest revenue per customer immediately: matches what operators already pay, so no price education needed. - Fastest to cash: it is the service I already perform, sellable tomorrow with no new product surface. - Deepest relationship and data: doing the work by hand surfaces exactly what to automate next. Negatives: - Does not compound: revenue is capped by my hours, the opposite of the product thesis. - Not investor-legible: a services business with one operator is not a venture story. - Pulls the roadmap toward more service and away from the product. Top competitors using this model: Capital Compliance (CapCom), Blue Wave Political, Elias Law Group, and every boutique compliance firm. Realistic 18-month customers + strategy: as a pure service I can personally carry maybe 8 to 12 committees before I am the bottleneck. Strategy: use the retainer as a wedge and a funded way to build the automation under each client, converting them to the cheaper SaaS as the product matures. Two price points: (a) parity: $2,500/month, the incumbent full retainer; (b) price-to-$1M: at $2,500/mo, $1M over 18 months needs an average of ~22 committees (about 44 at exit, ramped); at $2,000/mo, ~28 average (about 55 at exit). Both are more committees than one operator can service by hand, which is precisely why this cannot be the scaling model. Summary of findings. The primary model stays per-committee compliance SaaS at $1,000/month, because it is the only one of the three that compounds, is legible to investors, and matches how the buyer thinks (per committee, not per seat). The managed retainer is the right way to fund the build and stay close to the customer near-term. Interchange is real long-term upside that depends on the bank partnership going live. The honest read on the math: none of the three reaches $1M in 18 months on its own at a solo-founder customer count. $1M is a 24-to-36-month number, reached by stacking a SaaS base (roughly 40 to 60 committees at $1,000/mo, about $480k to $720k run-rate) on top of a handful of high-dollar retainers, with interchange as upside once the bank rails are live. The two price points I will actually test on the primary model are $1,000/month (at parity with the congressional-only incumbent) and a $1,500/month tier for multi-entity operators who want the reconciliation layer across several committees.

07 Revenue Interviews

Quartermint is B2B, so the bar is at least 2 customer interviews. I ran the pricing conversation with three operators from my Customer List and am reporting real signal from real conversations, anonymized by role, not invented numbers. Models tested with each: (1) per-committee compliance SaaS at $1,000/month, and (2) done-for-you managed retainer at $2,000 to $2,500/month. - J.R., PAC chair, active paying client (multi-entity treasury). - Prefers the product over the retainer: his pain is the judgment-heavy assembly, not data retrieval. Verbatim: "it's not the chase, because I can find information, it's the qualitative stuff." - Reacted to the entity-mapping feature with "this is gold" (three times) in a live session. - Pricing read: at $1,000/month for the filing product he is a clear yes; the value already exceeds that. - Anticipated lifetime value: a multi-cycle relationship, so $1,000/mo across a 2-plus-year horizon, plus higher-touch work during active campaigns. - How it changed my model: confirmed the product, not the service, is the thing he wants to keep paying for, which is the SaaS thesis. - Compliance operator who runs a multi-client firm (presidential-treasurer profile). - Lives inside the incumbent tools and watches constant churn, almost entirely on price and data quality. Verbatim: "NGP is trying to drop prices because they realize people are jumping ship right and left"; clients "move to Campaign Deputy or ISP because they can get it to $500 a month instead of $750 at a minimum"; on the cheap tool, "I get a lot of dupes... it builds up that database volume with a bunch of junk." - Pricing read: there is no loyalty in this market; clean data plus an affordable price is the wedge. $1,000/month for congressional is credible against a market that spans $500 cheap-and-junky to $7,500 full-stack. - Anticipated value: as a multi-client firm she is a reseller-shaped buyer, so lifetime value is many committees at $1,000/mo if the multi-tenant product earns her trust. - How it changed my model: pushed me to make clean, footed data the headline, because that is exactly what the cheap incumbents fail at. - Candidate-and-issue campaign strategist (runs his own committees). - Demand-pull, not a pitch: after I described the automated tool he asked me to build it against his own committees and offered live credentials. Verbatim: "build me a compliance tool, because I guarantee you that would save me a shitload of time." - Pricing read: he anchored the market for me. The incumbent (CapCom) runs ~$1,000/mo for congressional and ~$250/mo state, full retainers ~$2,500/mo, and a $1,000/mo product is something "you can sell to 20 races easily." - Anticipated value: multiple committees, multi-cycle, and he is a distribution node into other races. - How it changed my model: his "sell to 20 races easily" line is why $1,000/mo per committee is the right primary price, not a higher retainer that caps customer count. How the feedback changed my pricing model: I am holding $1,000 per committee per month as the primary price. The interviews confirmed (a) operators already churn on price, so an undercut is a real wedge, (b) the binding constraint is clean, footed data, not features, and (c) the per-committee unit is how buyers and resellers actually think. The managed retainer at $2,000 to $2,500 stays available for clients who want done-for-you, but as a wedge to fund the build, not as the scaling price.

08 Buyer Journey

Customer archetype: the operator responsible for a federal House or congressional committee's money and filings, which can be a compliance operator, a treasurer, a PAC principal, or a candidate acting as their own treasurer. Buyer journey, step by step: First encounter: 1. Warm referral from a trusted node: a strategist who picks the stack for many candidates, a compliance-firm principal (Blue Wave), or an Amalgamated Bank introduction. This is the dominant channel today; every customer so far is hand-sourced. 2. They see the live product (demos.quartermint.com) or watch me run a real filing on it. Becoming interested: 3. A 30-minute discovery call plus a quick audit of their current stack: which bank, ActBlue or Numero, which filing tool, how many hours per close. 4. The "email me every time money moves" moment: I show the real-time, reconciled view of their own money, the thing none of their current tools give them. 5. They see a filing assembled line by line from their own messy inputs, the proof the core promise works on real data. Deciding to buy: 6. A paid pilot scoped to one committee and one upcoming filing deadline, fixed term. The deadline is the forcing function: there is always a next filing. 7. The pilot either carries the filing cleanly or it does not. If it does, conversion is mostly automatic, because the alternative is going back to the midnight-spreadsheet workflow. Capturing payment to the business account: 8. Convert the pilot to a $1,000/month per-committee subscription, billed by card or ACH into Quartermint's operating account (Mercury). 9. Expand from one committee to the operator's other committees, which is the real growth motion: land one, then take the rest of the book. Cycle length and decision-makers: - Short-to-medium cycle: a single operator or self-treasurer can decide in days; a multi-client firm or a campaign with a manager and a lawyer in the loop takes a few weeks. - Others in the buying decision: for a firm, the principal; for a campaign, sometimes the campaign manager and outside counsel; the bank is an influencer, not an approver. - Renewals: it is a monthly subscription, but the real renewal cadence is the filing calendar. Every quarterly and 48-hour deadline is a moment the product re-earns the fee. - Trial: the paid pilot is the trial. I do not offer a free trial, because a free tool that touches regulated filings signals the wrong thing about trust. Steps to remove or shorten: - The slowest step is the bank-side work when an operator wants to move banking into the partner rails. I keep that off the critical path by selling the filing product first and treating the banking migration as a later, optional step. - The discovery-plus-audit can be compressed with a short intake form that captures their stack before the call, so the first conversation is about the filing, not fact-finding. - The highest-leverage accelerant is the deadline: I time the pilot to a real upcoming filing so urgency is built in rather than manufactured. Summary: the journey is referral, see the live product, discovery and stack audit, deadline-timed paid pilot on one committee, convert to per-committee subscription, expand across the book. It is founder-sold today, and the work ahead is making the early steps repeatable without me on every call.

15 Strategy Presentation Learning - Legal & Equity Questions

Concept that still confuses me after research: how to weigh QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock) at formation, specifically whether it is ever worth using a more complicated up-front structure to maximize QSBS rather than just forming a clean Delaware C-corp. What I understand: QSBS can exempt a large share of the gain on startup stock from federal tax if the stock is C-corp stock, was held five years, and the company met the size and other rules when the stock was issued. The default startup move, a Delaware C-corp with founder stock and an 83(b) election, puts you on the QSBS clock from day one and captures the standard exclusion. Why it still confuses me: there are advanced structures (for example, starting in an LLC and converting later, or stacking entities) that are described as ways to multiply or maximize the QSBS exemption beyond the standard exclusion. The guidance frames this as a real, large potential tax saving but also as significantly more complex and more expensive to set up and maintain, and it is genuinely unclear to me where the line sits: at what expected outcome does the extra complexity pay for itself, versus just forming the simple C-corp and taking standard QSBS treatment? My situation complicates it further, because the product IP already exists and was built before any entity, so I cannot tell whether the more complex path is even cleanly available to me or whether reaching for it would create more risk than it is worth. That tradeoff, the clean C-corp versus the complex QSBS-maximizing structure, and when the second is actually justified, is the specific thing I want to pressure-test with counsel.

16 Strategy Presentation Prep - Legal & Equity Questions

Specific legal questions for the Legal & Equity Session, scoped to Quartermint: 1. Entity and IP: I have built a working product and signed a paying engagement under my existing political-consulting LLC. What is the cleanest sequence to form a Delaware C-corp and assign that pre-existing IP into it so that my founder stock stays nominal-basis, there is no surprise taxable contribution event, and QSBS eligibility is preserved? Should the IP assignment happen before or after formation, and what specific document effects it? 2. Money-transmitter / fintech: Quartermint sits on top of money movement for political committees, ingesting bank and ActBlue data today and potentially routing card or ACH payments through a bank partner later. At what point does providing this treasury layer trigger money-transmitter licensing or agent-of-payee questions, and how do I structure the bank partnership so the bank, not Quartermint, holds the regulated money-movement role? 3. FEC vendor status: Does building software that prepares and assembles FEC filings for committees make Quartermint an FEC-reportable vendor, or create any registration or disclosure obligation of its own, separate from the committee's obligations? 4. Filing liability: My product prepares regulated filings but uses a human-gated submit; the treasurer reviews and signs, the software never auto-submits. If a filing the software helped prepare is later found to be wrong, where does liability fall between Quartermint and the committee, and what contract language (limitation of liability, "tool not advice," treasurer-of-record responsibility) actually holds up for a compliance software product? 5.
